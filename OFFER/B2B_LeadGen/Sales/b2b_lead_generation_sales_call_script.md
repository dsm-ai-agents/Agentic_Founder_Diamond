## Core sales principle

Do not sell “AI agents.”
Do not sell “lead lists.”
Do not sell “cold email.”

Sell the business outcome:

> “We help B2B companies create more qualified sales conversations by installing a repeatable outbound system: sharper targeting, cleaner data, AI-assisted prospect research, personalized outreach, and clear pipeline reporting.”

Use these words often:
- qualified sales conversations
- high-fit accounts
- cleaner pipeline
- repeatable outbound system
- signal-based prospecting
- AI-assisted research
- fewer bad-fit calls
- better pipeline visibility
- controlled test before scaling

Avoid these words unless the prospect uses them first:
- guaranteed leads
- guaranteed sales
- fully autonomous AI SDR
- blast campaign
- scrape everyone
- set-and-forget automation
- magic AI agent

## Quick call structure

1. Rapport and agenda — 3 minutes
2. Why they booked / why now — 5 minutes
3. Business and offer context — 5 minutes
4. Current pipeline diagnosis — 10 minutes
5. Current outbound process diagnosis — 10 minutes
6. Cost of the problem — 5 minutes
7. Desired future and success metrics — 5 minutes
8. Fit check — 5 minutes
9. Summary of the gap — 3 minutes
10. Recommendation — 7 minutes
11. Objections / decision process — 5 minutes
12. Close next step — 2 minutes

# 1. Opening the call

## 1.1 Warm greeting

Say:

“Hey [Name], thanks for taking the time today. Good to meet you.”

Say:

“Before we get into the business side, how’s your day going?”

If they respond casually, say:

“Nice. I’ll keep this useful and focused.”

## 1.2 Set the agenda

Say:

“Here’s how I suggest we use the call.”

Say:

“First, I’ll understand your business, your current pipeline, and how you’re generating sales conversations today.”

Say:

“Then I’ll ask a few questions around your outbound process: targeting, data quality, messaging, tools, follow-up, and reporting.”

Say:

“If I see a clear opportunity, I’ll recommend the best starting point — that could be a pipeline audit, a system buildout, or a managed outbound setup.”

Say:

“And if I don’t think we’re a fit, I’ll tell you directly and still try to leave you with useful next steps.”

Ask:

“Does that sound good?”

---

# 2. Why did they book?

## 2.1 Trigger question

Ask:

“What made you book this call?”

Follow-up:

“What stood out to you?”

Follow-up:

“Why is this important right now?”

Follow-up:

“What’s changed recently that made pipeline or outbound more urgent?”

## 2.2 If they came from a lead magnet

Say:

“I saw you downloaded the B2B lead generation playbook. Was there a specific part you were trying to solve — targeting, messaging, follow-ups, or tracking?”

Ask:

“Are you currently trying to build outbound from scratch, or improve something that already exists?”

## 2.3 If they came from LinkedIn / referral / DM

Ask:

“When you saw the idea of an AI-assisted outbound system, what problem did that connect to in your business?”

Ask:

“Are you mainly looking for more meetings, better quality meetings, or a more repeatable process?”

---

# 3. Understand the business

Ask:

“Can you give me the simple version of what you sell?”

Ask:

“Who is the main buyer?”

Ask:

“What type of companies are usually the best fit?”

Ask:

“What is your average deal size or customer value?”

Ask:

“How long is the typical sales cycle?”

Ask:

“How many new clients or deals are you trying to close per month or quarter?”

Ask:

“What growth target are you trying to hit this year?”

## 3.1 Clarify the offer

Ask:

“What problem do clients usually buy you to solve?”

Ask:

“What outcome are they expecting when they sign?”

Ask:

“What makes a prospect a bad fit?”

Ask:

“What makes a prospect a great fit?”

## 3.2 Check if outbound can work for them

Ask:

“Are your target buyers identifiable by industry, title, geography, company size, technology used, hiring signals, funding, events, or any other public signal?”

Ask:

“Can someone understand the pain from the outside, or does it only show up after a deep internal conversation?”

Ask:

“When a qualified prospect gets on a call, can your team usually convert them?”

---

# 4. Current pipeline diagnosis

## 4.1 Channel mix

Ask:

“How do you currently generate sales conversations?”

Ask:

“What percentage comes from referrals, inbound, outbound, paid ads, partnerships, events, or founder networking?”

Ask:

“Which channel is most reliable right now?”

Ask:

“Which channel is most unpredictable?”

## 4.2 Pipeline volume

Ask:

“How many qualified sales conversations do you need per month?”

Ask:

“How many are you getting now?”

Ask:

“How many of those are actually qualified — meaning they match the ICP, have the pain, and can move forward?”

Ask:

“What happens when that number drops for a month or two?”

## 4.3 Pipeline quality

Ask:

“Do you feel the main issue is not enough calls, or too many bad-fit calls?”

Ask:

“When meetings do not convert, what is the usual reason?”

Prompt options:

“Wrong company size?”

“Buyer too junior?”

“No urgent pain?”

“No budget path?”

“Just curious, not serious?”

“Bad timing?”

Ask:

“If you reviewed your last 10 sales calls, how many would you call real opportunities?”

---

# 5. Current outbound process diagnosis

## 5.1 Existing outbound

Ask:

“Are you doing outbound today?”

If yes, ask:

“What does the process look like from list building to booked meeting?”

If no, ask:

“Have you tried outbound before, or would this be the first serious attempt?”

## 5.2 Targeting

Ask:

“How do you decide which companies go on the list?”

Ask:

“What filters do you use before a lead hits sales?”

Ask:

“Do you have a clear disqualification rule, or does almost anyone get added if they look close enough?”

Ask:

“What buying signals do you currently track?”

Examples to offer:

“Hiring activity, funding, new locations, job postings, new leadership, tech stack changes, content activity, competitor usage, new product launches, expansion, events, or regulatory changes.”

## 5.3 Data quality

Ask:

“Where are leads coming from today?”

Examples:

“Apollo, LinkedIn Sales Navigator, Clay, ZoomInfo, industry directories, events, VAs, referrals, old CRM data?”

Ask:

“How confident are you in the quality of the data?”

Ask:

“Are you seeing issues with wrong titles, bad emails, bounced contacts, generic inboxes, or outdated company data?”

Ask:

“Does bad data ever create wasted sales calls or damage trust with prospects?”

## 5.4 Messaging

Ask:

“How personalized is your outreach today?”

Ask:

“Is the message based on a real trigger, or is it mostly generic role-based copy?”

Ask:

“What message angles have worked best so far?”

Ask:

“What message angles have failed?”

Ask:

“Do prospects understand the outcome before the call, or does your team have to educate them from zero?”

## 5.5 Deliverability and compliance

Ask:

“What sending setup are you using?”

Ask:

“Are you sending from your primary domain or separate outbound domains?”

Ask:

“Are SPF, DKIM, DMARC, warmup, volume control, bounce tracking, and opt-out handling set up properly?”

Ask:

“Have you had any issues with deliverability, spam placement, domain reputation, or high bounce rates?”

Say:

“I ask because bad outreach can damage more than reply rates. It can hurt your domain, your brand, and your team’s credibility.”

## 5.6 Tools and operations

Ask:

“What tools are you currently using for prospecting, enrichment, outreach, CRM, and reporting?”

Ask:

“Who owns the process internally?”

Ask:

“Who builds the list?”

Ask:

“Who writes the copy?”

Ask:

“Who handles replies?”

Ask:

“What happens when someone replies positively?”

Ask:

“Does that handoff get tracked in the CRM?”

## 5.7 Reporting

Ask:

“What are you tracking today?”

Examples:

“Opens, replies, positive replies, meetings booked, meeting quality, opportunities created, pipeline value, closed revenue?”

Ask:

“Can you tell which segment, list source, or message is creating real opportunities?”

Ask:

“Or is the report mostly activity — opens, replies, and meetings?”

Say:

“The goal is not to make the report look busy. The goal is to see which campaigns create commercially useful conversations.”

# 6. Find the bottleneck

Say:

“Let me narrow this down.”

Ask:

“If you had to pick the biggest bottleneck right now, is it targeting, data, messaging, deliverability, follow-up, reporting, or team capacity?”

Ask:

“What makes that the biggest bottleneck?”

Ask:

“Who is currently spending time dealing with it?”

Ask:

“How often does it show up?”

Ask:

“What does it prevent your team from doing?”

## 6.1 Three-task version

Ask:

“If you could eliminate or systematize three repetitive pipeline tasks tomorrow, what would they be?”

Follow-up:

“Why those three?”

Follow-up:

“Who performs them right now?”

Follow-up:

“What happens when they are not done consistently?”

---

# 7. Quantify the cost

## 7.1 Time cost

Ask:

“How many hours per week does the team spend on list building, prospect research, data cleanup, personalization, follow-up, and reporting?”

Ask:

“What is the approximate hourly cost of the people doing that work?”

Say:

“So if we estimate [X] hours per week at [Y] cost, that is roughly [X × Y] per week in manual pipeline work.”

Ask:

“Does that sound directionally right?”

## 7.2 Opportunity cost

Ask:

“What is one qualified sales conversation worth to you?”

Ask:

“What is one new customer worth?”

Ask:

“If you were getting [number] more qualified conversations per month, what could that mean commercially?”

Ask:

“What does it cost you when the team spends time on bad-fit calls?”

## 7.3 Cost of doing nothing

Ask:

“If nothing changes in the next 3 months, what happens?”

Ask:

“If nothing changes in the next 12 months, what does that cost the business?”

Ask:

“What does inconsistent pipeline force you to do — discounting, chasing referrals, overworking founder sales, cutting growth plans, hiring too early?”

---

# 8. Desired future

Ask:

“If this problem was solved 90 days from now, what would be different?”

Ask:

“What would your ideal outbound system look like?”

Ask:

“What would your team no longer have to manually fight with?”

Ask:

“What would you want to see in a weekly report to trust that the system is working?”

Ask:

“What would make you say, ‘This was worth it’?”

## 8.1 Define success metrics

Ask:

“Which metric matters most to you: qualified meetings, opportunity creation, lower CAC, less founder prospecting, cleaner CRM data, faster segment testing, or better sales team productivity?”

Ask:

“What number would make this meaningful?”

Examples:

“5 qualified meetings per month?”

“10 sales conversations with decision-makers?”

“500 clean prospects in a priority segment?”

“Three tested outbound angles?”

“A working campaign your team can run internally?”

# 9. Fit check

Say:

“I’m going to be direct on fit because outbound is not right for every business.”

Ask:

“Is your offer already converting when you get in front of the right buyer?”

Ask:

“Is the deal size high enough to justify outbound investment?”

Ask:

“Can your team respond quickly when prospects reply?”

Ask:

“Are you comfortable testing and iterating rather than expecting one campaign to solve everything immediately?”

Ask:

“Are you looking for a real pipeline system, or mainly a cheap pay-per-meeting vendor?”

## 9.1 Good fit indicators

Good fit if they say:
- clear B2B offer
- average deal value is meaningful
- founder or sales team can close
- current pipeline is inconsistent
- existing tools are underused
- they care about quality, not only volume
- they can approve copy and respond to replies
- they are open to a controlled 30-day test

## 9.2 Poor fit indicators

Poor fit if they say:
- offer is unclear
- very low-ticket sale
- wants guaranteed sales
- no one can follow up
- wants bulk spam
- only wants pure pay-per-meeting
- cannot describe ICP
- expects AI to fix a weak offer

If poor fit, say:

“I don’t think the full outbound system is the right next step yet. The first issue is [ICP / offer / sales process / follow-up]. I’d rather fix that before selling you outbound execution.”

---

# 10. Summarize the gap

Say:

“Let me summarize what I’m hearing, and you tell me if I’m off.”

Use this structure:

“Right now, your current state is: [current pipeline situation].”

“The main bottleneck seems to be: [targeting / data / messaging / deliverability / reporting / capacity].”

“The cost of that is: [time wasted / bad-fit calls / inconsistent revenue / missed opportunities / tool spend / founder time].”

“What you actually want is: [qualified conversations / cleaner pipeline / repeatable outbound / better reporting / segment testing].”

“And the gap is not just more leads. It is a system that can consistently identify the right accounts, research them, personalize outreach, route replies, and show what is working.”

Ask:

“Is that an accurate summary?”

If yes, continue.

If no, ask:

“What would you change?”

---

# 11. Present the opportunity

Say:

“Based on what you shared, I would not frame this as buying cold email or buying AI tools.”

Say:

“The opportunity is to install the operating layer between your market, your tools, and your sales team.”

Say:

“That means we define the right accounts, clean the data, research the decision-makers, write segmented outreach, launch controlled campaigns, track replies and opportunities, and improve based on real sales feedback.”

Say:

“The goal is not volume for the sake of volume. The goal is qualified movement — conversations your team actually wants.”

# 12. Recommendation paths

Choose the path based on the call.

## 12.1 Path A — Pipeline Audit

Best when:
- prospect is skeptical
- current outbound exists but is messy
- they are not ready for a full build
- you need a low-friction first sale

Say:

“My recommendation is to start with a Pipeline Audit.”

Say:

“In 7 days, we review your ICP, current outbound stack, lead sources, deliverability, CRM handoff, and reporting.”

Say:

“You’ll get 3–5 campaign opportunities and a quick-win action plan showing where the pipeline is leaking and what to fix first.”

Say:

“This is the safest starting point if you want clarity before implementation.”

Price language:

“The audit is [insert price]. If you decide to move into implementation after that, we can apply part or all of the audit fee toward the buildout if that makes sense for the engagement.”

Close:

“Would starting with the audit feel like the right next step?”

## 12.2 Path B — AI Outbound Engine buildout

Best when:
- prospect needs system built
- no strong outbound infrastructure exists
- they have clear ICP and offer
- they want done-for-you setup

Say:

“My recommendation is the AI Outbound Engine buildout.”

Say:

“This is a 14–21 day setup where we build the first working version of your outbound system.”

Say:

“It includes ICP refinement, 500–1,000 researched prospects, decision-maker enrichment, email verification, 2–3 segmented campaigns, AI-assisted personalization, campaign setup, and a reporting sheet.”

Say:

“The goal is to go from scattered prospecting to a controlled campaign system your team can understand, approve, and improve.”

Price language:

“The buildout is [insert price]. For first clients or pilot engagements, the range can be [insert pilot price]. After proof and scope expansion, this typically moves into [insert standard setup price].”

Close:

“If we mapped the first segment and campaign this week, would you be ready to start the buildout?”

## 12.3 Path C — Managed AI Pipeline

Best when:
- prospect wants ongoing execution
- they do not want to manage tools
- they need monthly prospect sourcing and campaign iteration
- they have meaningful deal value

Say:

“My recommendation is the Managed AI Pipeline.”

Say:

“This is for companies that do not just want the system built — they want us to run and improve it every month.”

Say:

“Each month we handle prospect sourcing, enrichment, verification, campaign copy iterations, personalization, sending infrastructure monitoring, weekly optimization, reply triage, CRM updates, dashboard reporting, and qualified meeting handoff.”

Say:

“This is best if you want consistency and do not want your founder or sales team stuck managing the whole outbound machine.”

Price language:

“The managed retainer is [insert monthly price], with setup handled separately or included depending on scope. For the right fit, we can also discuss a qualified-meeting or qualified-opportunity bonus on top of the retainer.”

Close:

“Do you want a team to build the system and hand it over, or do you want us to operate it with you monthly?”

# 13. Explain the first 30 days

“Here’s what the first 30 days would look like.”

## Week 1 — Strategy and setup

Say:

“Week 1 is strategy and setup. We clarify the ICP, segments, buying triggers, disqualification rules, tools, campaign hypothesis, and what your team needs to approve before launch.”

## Week 2 — Data and workflow

Say:

“Week 2 is data and workflow. We build the account list, enrich decision-makers, verify emails, prepare CRM fields, and create the AI-assisted research workflow.”

## Week 3 — Messaging and launch

Say:

“Week 3 is messaging and launch. We write the segmented sequences, create personalization logic, set up the outreach tool, and launch a controlled test batch.”

## Week 4 — Optimize and report

Say:

“Week 4 is optimization and reporting. We review replies, bounces, meeting quality, copy performance, segment performance, and decide what to scale, pause, or improve.”

Say:

“The important thing is we do not blindly scale volume. We validate quality first.”

# 15. Objection handling

## 15.1 “Do you guarantee meetings?”

Say:

“I don’t guarantee meetings because meeting volume depends on offer strength, market timing, list quality, deliverability, and sales follow-up.”

Say:

“What I can guarantee is the delivery of a researched, measurable outbound system: clear ICP, clean prospect data, verified contacts, segmented campaigns, approved messaging, controlled launch, and reporting.”

Say:

“I’d rather be honest about that than promise calendar volume that may not create real opportunities.”

Ask:

“Are you optimizing for any meeting, or for qualified conversations that sales can actually use?”

## 15.2 “We tried cold email and it did not work.”

Say:

“That’s common. Usually cold email fails before the email is written.”

Say:

“The list is too broad, the data is weak, the message has no trigger, the sending setup is risky, or follow-up is inconsistent.”

Ask:

“When you tried it, where do you think it broke — targeting, data, copy, deliverability, or follow-up?”

Say:

“What we build is not just another email campaign. It is a controlled outbound system designed to learn which segment and message creates qualified movement.”

## 15.3 “We already use Apollo / Clay / Instantly / Smartlead.”

Say:

“That’s good. Those tools can be useful.”

Say:

“But tools give you data and automation. They do not automatically give you ICP clarity, targeting logic, message-market fit, deliverability discipline, reply routing, CRM handoff, or sales feedback loops.”

Say:

“We operate the layer between the tools and the pipeline outcome.”

Ask:

“Are the tools currently producing qualified opportunities, or mostly activity?”

## 15.4 “Can we just buy a lead list?”

Say:

“You can, but a lead list by itself is not a pipeline system.”

Say:

“The question is whether the accounts are high-fit, whether the contacts are the right decision-makers, whether the emails are verified, whether there is a reason to reach out now, and whether the message connects to a real pain.”

Say:

“If you only need raw data, we may not be the best fit. If you want a usable outbound workflow, that’s where we can help.”

## 15.5 “This feels expensive.”

Say:

“That’s fair. It should feel expensive if the deal value or urgency is not there.”

Ask:

“What is one qualified opportunity worth to you?”

Ask:

“What does one closed customer usually generate?”

Ask:

“What does inconsistent pipeline cost you over a quarter?”

Say:

“If the numbers do not support the investment, I would recommend starting with a smaller audit or sprint.”

## 15.6 “Can we do performance-only?”

Say:

“I usually do not start with pure performance-only because it creates the wrong incentives and ignores the setup work required: ICP, data, infrastructure, copy, systems, and reporting.”

Say:

“I’m open to a structure with a retainer plus a qualified-meeting or qualified-opportunity bonus once the qualification criteria are clear.”

Ask:

“How would you define a qualified meeting so both sides agree it is valuable?”

## 15.7 “We need to think about it.”

Say:

“Of course. What specifically do you need to think through?”

If vague, ask:

“Is it the priority, the budget, the timing, trust in the process, or internal approval?”

Then respond to the real issue.

Say:

“The worst next step would be leaving this vague. If it is useful, we can agree on either a clear yes, a clear no, or a small audit as a lower-risk first step.”

## 15.8 “Send me a proposal.”

Say:

“Happy to. Before I send it, I want to make sure it reflects the right scope.”

Ask:

“Which option feels closest: audit, buildout, or managed execution?”

Ask:

“Who else needs to review it?”

Ask:

“What would make the proposal an easy yes?”

Ask:

“When should we review it together?”

## 15.9 “We need more volume.”

Say:

“Volume helps only after the targeting and message are working.”

Say:

“If we scale a weak list or generic copy, we just create more noise, more bounces, and more bad-fit calls.”

Say:

“My recommendation is to validate one narrow segment first, then increase volume once we know it produces quality conversations.”

## 15.10 “Will AI write all the messages?”

Say:

“AI helps with research, segmentation, and personalization angles. But we do not blindly let AI send brand-sensitive outreach without review.”

Say:

“The safest workflow is AI-assisted, human-approved.”

Say:

“That protects your brand while still saving research and personalization time.”

# 16. Segment-specific question paths

## 16.1 B2B SaaS / Software companies

Use this when the prospect sells software or SaaS.

Ask:

“Are you trying to book more demos, test a new vertical, lower CAC, or support founder-led sales?”

Ask:

“Are your current demos with accounts that match your ICP, or are reps spending time with junior contacts and low-intent buyers?”

Ask:

“What is your current paid acquisition cost, and are you trying to reduce dependence on paid channels?”

Ask:

“Do you need meetings with one buyer, or a full buying committee?”

Ask:

“Are you tracking outreach activity all the way to opportunity and revenue?”

Positioning line:

“We help founder-led SaaS teams test outbound segments quickly before hiring SDRs or scaling paid acquisition.”

## 16.2 Professional services / consulting / agencies

Use this when the prospect sells services, consulting, or agency work.

Ask:

“Are referrals coming in consistently, or do they come in waves?”

Ask:

“What happens two or three months after a quiet referral month?”

Ask:

“Are your calls with decision-makers who can sign off on a retainer, or people looking for free advice?”

Ask:

“How important is it that outbound protects your expert positioning and does not make the brand look cheap?”

Ask:

“Do you need a predictable way to book conversations in a specific niche?”

Positioning line:

“We help agencies and professional services firms create a second pipeline engine so revenue is not dependent only on referrals, networking, or founder prospecting.”

## 16.3 Recruiting / staffing firms

Use this when the prospect is a recruiter or staffing firm.

Ask:

“Are you more focused on finding client companies, candidates, or both?”

Ask:

“Do you currently use hiring signals like job postings, team expansion, funding, or new locations?”

Ask:

“What roles or industries are most profitable for you?”

Ask:

“Are you reaching companies actively hiring, or broad lists of HR contacts?”

Positioning line:

“We find companies actively hiring in your niche and turn those hiring signals into client conversations.”

## 16.4 Industrial / manufacturing / logistics / distribution

Use this when the prospect sells into operational or industrial buyers.

Ask:

“Which buyer roles matter most — procurement, plant, operations, supply chain, finance, or leadership?”

Ask:

“What triggers usually create demand — expansion, equipment upgrades, supplier problems, downtime, regulatory changes, or new projects?”

Ask:

“Does your sales team need RFQs, site visits, distributor conversations, or technical discovery calls?”

Ask:

“Are geography, facility size, certifications, volume, or application requirements important qualification filters?”

Positioning line:

“We identify accounts with the right operational fit and buying triggers so your sales team is not chasing generic contacts with no reason to buy.”

# 17. Closing scripts

## 17.1 Direct close

Say:

“Based on everything we discussed, I think the best next step is [audit / buildout / managed pipeline].”

Say:

“The reason is [specific reason from call].”

Say:

“If we start this week, the first milestone would be [ICP workshop / audit kickoff / list build / campaign map].”

Ask:

“Do you want to move forward with that?”

## 17.2 Soft close

Ask:

“On a scale of 1 to 10, how aligned does this feel with what you need?”

If less than 8, ask:

“What would need to be clearer to make it an 8 or 9?”

If 8 or above, ask:

“What would be the next step on your side to get this started?”

## 17.3 Pilot close

Say:

“If you do not want to commit to ongoing execution immediately, we can start with one narrow pilot.”

Say:

“One segment, one buyer, one core pain, 500–1,000 prospects, 2–3 message angles, controlled launch, and clear reporting.”

Ask:

“Would a focused pilot be the right way to validate this before scaling?”

## 17.4 Audit close

Say:

“The lowest-risk next step is the Pipeline Audit.”

Say:

“It gives you clarity even if you do not move forward with implementation.”

Ask:

“Should we start there?”

## 17.5 Calendar close

Say:

“Let’s put the next step on the calendar now so this does not become a vague follow-up.”

Ask:

“What does your calendar look like for [day/time]?”

# 18. Decision-process questions

# 20. Quick qualification scorecard

Score each from 1–5.

1. Clear B2B offer
2. Clear ICP
3. Average deal value supports outbound investment
4. Decision-makers are identifiable
5. Buying signals can be found externally
6. Existing pipeline pain is urgent
7. Sales team/founder can close qualified calls
8. They value quality over volume
9. They can approve and respond quickly
10. They have budget or clear ROI case

Interpretation:
- 40–50: strong fit
- 30–39: possible fit, start with audit or pilot
- 20–29: weak fit, fix fundamentals first
- Below 20: do not sell implementation
